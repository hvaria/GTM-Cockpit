Aerospace · Military · Space (AMS)
Hypersonics structural testing
VPG's UC system (beta at University of Alabama) tests ceramic materials used in hypersonic missiles. Kratos won $1.45B MACH-TB Phase 2 hypersonic test contract Jan 2025 — direct demand signal.
High-temperature flight test
Self-temperature-compensating foil strain gages for hypersonic vehicle skin instrumentation and airframe fatigue. Lockheed won $1B Navy hypersonic contract June 2025.
AMS instrument calibration
Precision foil resistors and reference load cells for avionics test equipment. AMS book-to-bill exceeded 1.1 in Q2 2025 per VPG earnings — demand recovery confirmed.
Source: VPG Q2–Q4 FY2025 earnings · AIAA Hypersonics review Jan 2026 · GlobeNewsWire · Aerospace America
Test & Measurement
Servohydraulic fatigue test rigs

Advanced materials characterization
UC system for band testing nonconductive ceramics — University of Alabama beta is a pilot for broader market entry. Adjacent to growing hypersonics/defense materials programs.
Precision resistors in instruments
Foil resistors outperform metal film in calibration instruments — stability, TCR, load life. Directly cited as revenue growth driver in VPG Q4 2025 earnings.

How one article becomes 3 GTM assets
VPG publishes detailed technical content on topics like tactile sensing and precision measurement. This is how a single technical article maps to three distinct outreach formats — each written for a different stage and audience.
Input: Technical Article
Topic: "How VPG's custom foil strain gage arrays are enabling dexterous robotic hands — sub-gram resolution at the fingertip level."

Audience: Robotics engineers and systems integrators at humanoid robot OEMs.
Output 1: LinkedIn Post
Framed as an observation, not a pitch: "Elon Musk said the robotic hand is the hardest part. VPG's foil gage arrays are addressing exactly this..." — hooks with a known reference, delivers technical insight, closes with a question that invites comment.
Output 2: Cold Email + Follow-up
Email names the specific application (fingertip force sensing vs. wrist T&F), references VPG's confirmed design-ins, asks a single specific engineering question: "Is your current end-effector sensing architecture spec'd below the wrist level?"
